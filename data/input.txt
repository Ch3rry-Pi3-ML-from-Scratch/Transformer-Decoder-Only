ACT I

SAGE:
The lamp is low, yet still the page is bright.

APPRENTICE:
Then let me read until the morning comes.

SAGE:
Read slowly. A small sign may hide a rule.

APPRENTICE:
I see the marks, but not the path between.

SAGE:
The path is made by walking line by line.

APPRENTICE:
And if I lose the thread before the end?

SAGE:
Return to what is known, and start again.

SCRIBE:
The ink is dry, the margin waits for notes.

APPRENTICE:
Write this: a model learns by many tries.

SAGE:
Write also this: a guess must meet a loss.

SCRIBE:
I write it plain, so no one misses it.

APPRENTICE:
What is a token, when the text is small?

SAGE:
A token here is but a single sign.

APPRENTICE:
One letter walks alone into the net?

SAGE:
Alone at first, then joined with place and time.

SCRIBE:
The first lesson is counted in the dark.

APPRENTICE:
Then every letter needs a little home.

SAGE:
The table gives each letter such a home.

APPRENTICE:
And every place receives a second mark?

SAGE:
Yes. Order matters. Night is not thing.

APPRENTICE:
You mean that "star" and "rats" are not the same.

SAGE:
The letters match, but meaning turns around.

SCRIBE:
I mark this down beside the candle flame.

ACT II

CARTOGRAPHER:
Bring me the map with rivers still unnamed.

RUNNER:
The map is torn, but all the roads remain.

CARTOGRAPHER:
Then we shall trace the roads from town to town.

RUNNER:
May every town look forward down the road?

CARTOGRAPHER:
No. Each town sees the towns already passed.

RUNNER:
Why must the future stay behind a veil?

CARTOGRAPHER:
Because the game is guessing what comes next.

RUNNER:
If I could see the next town, I would cheat.

CARTOGRAPHER:
So we draw masks across the future road.

SCRIBE:
The mask is lower triangular and true.

RUNNER:
I do not love that phrase, but hear its shape.

CARTOGRAPHER:
At step one, look only at step one.

RUNNER:
At step two, look at one and two together.

CARTOGRAPHER:
At step three, look at one and two and three.

RUNNER:
The pattern grows, but never looks ahead.

CARTOGRAPHER:
Now you have seen the law of causal sight.

SAGE:
A law of sight becomes a law of scores.

APPRENTICE:
The scores are changed before the softmax comes.

SAGE:
The hidden future falls to minus night.

SCRIBE:
And zero chance is born from endless cold.

ACT III

APPRENTICE:
What does attention seek inside a line?

SAGE:
It asks three questions with three learned doors.

APPRENTICE:
What am I seeking? What do others hold?

SAGE:
And what should they return if I attend?

APPRENTICE:
These are the queries, keys, and values then.

SAGE:
Yes. Query meets a key and forms a score.

APPRENTICE:
The score is scaled before it grows too sharp.

SAGE:
Divide by root of width, and keep it calm.

APPRENTICE:
Then mask the future, soften what remains.

SAGE:
Then use the weights to blend the values in.

SCRIBE:
I write the shapes to hold the thought in place.

SAGE:
Batch, time, and channel march in order here.

APPRENTICE:
The head returns a thinner hidden stream.

SAGE:
Many heads return, and then their streams are joined.

APPRENTICE:
Joined side by side, not added into one.

SAGE:
Then one projection mixes all their views.

SCRIBE:
The shape returns, so residuals can pass.

APPRENTICE:
The old stream plus the new stream moves ahead.

SAGE:
That is the bridge across the depth of blocks.

ACT IV

BUILDER:
I have a block with two clear chambers built.

INSPECTOR:
Name them, so the work can be reviewed.

BUILDER:
First comes attention, wrapped in normal form.

INSPECTOR:
And after that?

BUILDER:
The feed-forward room.

INSPECTOR:
Does every room preserve the outer shape?

BUILDER:
It must, or else the skip path cannot meet.

INSPECTOR:
Show me the order of the moving stream.

BUILDER:
Normalise, attend, then add the old.

INSPECTOR:
And then?

BUILDER:
Normalise, transform, then add again.

APPRENTICE:
The network widens, bends, and narrows back.

SAGE:
Four times the width is common for that room.

SCRIBE:
The block is small, but can be stacked in rows.

BUILDER:
One block remembers little by itself.

INSPECTOR:
Two blocks may find a pattern in the smoke.

BUILDER:
More blocks may learn, but training costs us time.

SAGE:
Begin with two, and let the loss report.

APPRENTICE:
So judgement comes from numbers, not from hope.

SAGE:
From numbers first, and samples second after.

ACT V

TRAINER:
Bring forth the corpus and divide the stream.

APPRENTICE:
How much is held aside for validation?

TRAINER:
A tenth is kept to test what was not trained.

APPRENTICE:
The rest becomes the ground for many steps.

TRAINER:
Each window has an input and a target.

APPRENTICE:
The target is the input shifted left.

TRAINER:
Yes. Every place predicts the next small sign.

APPRENTICE:
The model speaks in logits, not in words.

TRAINER:
The loss will shape those logits into skill.

APPRENTICE:
We flatten batch and time before the loss.

TRAINER:
Then cross entropy compares each row.

SAGE:
Clear gradients first, then let the backward pass.

TRAINER:
Then AdamW will nudge the weights along.

APPRENTICE:
How will I know the learning has begun?

TRAINER:
The training loss should fall across the steps.

SAGE:
The validation loss must also be watched.

SCRIBE:
A falling train loss alone may flatter us.

APPRENTICE:
If validation rises, the model memorises.

TRAINER:
Then train less, shrink the net, or gather text.

ACT VI

ORACLE:
Give me a prompt, and I will ask the model.

APPRENTICE:
The prompt is short: SAGE:

ORACLE:
The model sees only ids, not names.

APPRENTICE:
Yet names become ids through the token table.

ORACLE:
The current context enters as a row.

APPRENTICE:
The logits return for every position there.

ORACLE:
But only the final position chooses next.

APPRENTICE:
Why not use all positions at generation time?

ORACLE:
The earlier positions predict earlier futures.

APPRENTICE:
Only the last one stands before the unknown.

ORACLE:
Exactly. Take its scores across the vocab.

APPRENTICE:
Temperature may sharpen or soften them.

ORACLE:
Then sample one sign, or choose the greatest score.

APPRENTICE:
Append the sign and call the model again.

ORACLE:
One token at a time, the line is made.

SCRIBE:
The machine writes slowly, but the loop is simple.

SAGE:
Slow loops teach more than hidden magic does.

APPRENTICE:
Then let the first strange sample be enough.

SAGE:
Enough to learn, and not enough to boast.

ACT VII

NIGHT WATCH:
The square is quiet, and the bell is late.

LAMP KEEPER:
Still I can hear the scratching of the pen.

NIGHT WATCH:
What do they build beyond the library door?

LAMP KEEPER:
A little engine made of numbered words.

NIGHT WATCH:
Will it know the moon?

LAMP KEEPER:
It will know the letters.

NIGHT WATCH:
Will it know the city?

LAMP KEEPER:
It will know the patterns.

NIGHT WATCH:
Will it know the heart?

LAMP KEEPER:
Not yet. It learns the next mark first.

SAGE:
Do not ask a small model for a soul.

APPRENTICE:
Ask it instead to show the shape it learned.

SCRIBE:
In repeated names, it finds repeated turns.

APPRENTICE:
In line breaks, it finds the breath of speech.

SAGE:
In punctuation, it finds a little rhythm.

NIGHT WATCH:
Then even small text has a pattern hidden.

LAMP KEEPER:
All ordered things invite prediction.

ACT VIII

APPRENTICE:
I trained the model through a hundred steps.

SAGE:
What did the loss reveal?

APPRENTICE:
It fell, then shook, then fell a little more.

SAGE:
That is the common weather of first runs.

APPRENTICE:
The sample speaks in broken stage directions.

SAGE:
Good. Broken structure is structure still.

APPRENTICE:
It learned that speakers end with colons.

SAGE:
It learned that lines are short and often paired.

APPRENTICE:
It learned some words, but loses longer thought.

SAGE:
A tiny model with tiny data will do that.

TRAINER:
Increase the text before increasing pride.

SCRIBE:
Increase tests before increasing tricks.

ORACLE:
Increase patience before increasing scale.

APPRENTICE:
Then the next step is plain enough to see.

SAGE:
Fill the corpus, train the loop, inspect the sample.

APPRENTICE:
Then change one thing and measure what it means.

SAGE:
That is experiment. That is the work.

SCRIBE:
I close the page, but leave the lamp alight.

APPRENTICE:
Tomorrow I will ask the model again.
